Section 01

Introduction

Creatine Kinase (CK), also known as Creatine Phosphokinase (CPK), is a cytoplasmic and mitochondrial enzyme that plays a central role in energy metabolism within cells that have high and fluctuating energy demands.

Energy Metabolism

Catalyzes the reversible transfer of a phosphate group from ATP to creatine, forming phosphocreatine (PCr) — the primary energy buffer in muscle cells.

Dimeric Enzyme

CK is a dimer composed of two subunits (M and B). Different combinations produce three cytoplasmic isoenzymes: CK-MM, CK-MB, and CK-BB.

Clinical Biomarker

Elevated CK is a sensitive indicator of muscle damage, myocardial infarction, and various neuromuscular disorders, making it a critical diagnostic enzyme.

Tissue Distribution

Found predominantly in skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle, and brain tissue — each containing characteristic isoenzyme patterns that aid tissue-specific diagnosis.

Structure of Creatine Kinase

CK is a homodimeric or heterodimeric enzyme. Each monomer has a molecular weight of approximately 43 kDa, forming a functional dimer of ~86 kDa. The two subunit types — M (Muscle) and B (Brain) — combine to create distinct isoenzymes.

Section 03

CK Isoenzymes

The three cytoplasmic isoenzymes of CK differ in subunit composition, tissue origin, and electrophoretic mobility. They are separated clinically because each reflects damage to a specific tissue type.

Skeletal Muscle Isoenzyme

Brain Isoenzyme

~0%

undetectable in normal adult serum

  • Composed of two B subunits (BB homodimer)
  • Predominant in brain, lung, smooth muscle, and thyroid

Mitochondrial CK Isoforms

Two additional mitochondrial isoforms exist: sarcomeric MtCK (sMtCK) — found in striated muscle mitochondria — and ubiquitous MtCK (uMtCK) — found in non-muscle mitochondria. These form octamers in the mitochondrial intermembrane space, coupling oxidative phosphorylation with PCr production.

Source: NCBI Bookshelf: Creatine Kinase Isoenzymes; Braunwald's Heart Disease (12th ed.), Chapter 14; Tietz, 2019, pp. 419–425
Section 04

Function & Mechanism

CK catalyzes a fully reversible phosphotransfer reaction that is critical for maintaining cellular ATP levels in tissues with fluctuating energy demands.

Temporal Energy Buffer

PCr serves as the immediate phosphate donor to regenerate ATP during rapid energy bursts (first 5–10 seconds of maximal exercise).

🔄

Spatial Energy Transfer

Mitochondrial CK converts ATP (produced at inner membrane) to PCr, which diffuses to cytoplasmic sites of ATP utilization — the "PCr shuttle."

⚖️

Cellular Homeostasis

Maintains stable ADP/ATP ratio, preventing inhibition of ATP-consuming enzymes and protecting against metabolic stress.

🧠

Brain Energy Metabolism

CK-BB provides rapid ATP buffering in neurons during synaptic transmission and intense firing activity.

💪

Muscle Contraction

PCr-CK system allows sustained high-frequency muscle contractions beyond what ATP stores alone could support.

Source: Wallimann T, Tokarska-Schlattner M, Schlattner U. (2011). Biochem J, 443(2):203-320; Lehninger's Principles of Biochemistry (7th ed.), Chapter 23
Section 05

Clinical Significance

CK measurement is one of the most widely ordered enzyme tests in clinical medicine. It is particularly valuable in diagnosing conditions involving muscle cell membrane disruption, as CK is normally confined intracellularly.

🫀

Acute Myocardial Infarction (AMI)

CK-MB is a classical cardiac biomarker. It rises within 3–6 hours of myocardial injury, peaks at 12–24 hours, and normalizes within 48–72 hours. A CK-MB/total CK ratio >6% is highly suggestive of myocardial origin. Although largely superseded by troponin for AMI diagnosis, CK-MB remains valuable for estimating infarct size and detecting reinfarction.

Rhabdomyolysis

Massive skeletal muscle breakdown leads to CK levels exceeding 10,000–100,000 U/L. Risk of acute kidney injury from myoglobinuria.

CNS Injury / Stroke

CK-BB elevation indicates blood-brain barrier disruption from stroke, head trauma, hypoxic encephalopathy, or brain surgery.

Muscular Dystrophies

Duchenne MD: CK may be 100× normal — the earliest biomarker, often detectable before clinical symptoms.

Inflammatory Myopathies

Polymyositis and dermatomyositis cause significant CK-MM elevation, correlating with disease activity and treatment response.

Drug-Induced Myopathy

Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors) can cause myopathy and rhabdomyolysis; CK monitoring guides statin therapy safety.

Ω

Hypothyroidism

Thyroid hormone deficiency impairs myocyte membrane integrity; CK-MM may be moderately to markedly elevated even without symptoms.

Section 07

Causes of Abnormal CK Levels

Understanding the causes of elevated and decreased CK levels is essential for accurate clinical interpretation and differential diagnosis.

Causes of Increased CK

  • Acute Myocardial Infarction (AMI) — CK-MB elevation; specific cardiac marker
  • Rhabdomyolysis — Massive muscle breakdown; CK may exceed 100,000 U/L
  • Intense Physical Exercise — Transient elevation of CK-MM from micro-tears
  • Intramuscular Injections — Local muscle trauma causes CK-MM release
  • Muscular Dystrophies — Duchenne MD: up to 100× normal; Becker MD: moderately elevated
  • Inflammatory Myopathies — Polymyositis, dermatomyositis
  • Drug-Induced Myopathy — Statins, fibrates, colchicine, corticosteroids, cocaine
  • CNS Injury / Stroke — CK-BB elevation indicating neuronal damage
  • Hypothyroidism — Thyroid deficiency impairs myocyte function
  • Electrical Injury / Burns — Direct thermal and electrical muscle damage
  • Viral Myositis — Influenza, COVID-19, enterovirus myopathy
  • Post-surgical / Trauma — Any skeletal muscle trauma or surgery

Causes of Decreased CK

  • Prolonged Bed Rest / Immobilization — Reduced muscle mass and activity lowers CK
  • Hyperthyroidism — Excess thyroid hormone accelerates CK clearance
  • Pregnancy (1st & 2nd Trimester) — Dilutional effect and hormonal changes reduce CK
  • 🫀
    Liver Disease / Cirrhosis — Reduced hepatic clearance may paradoxically lower circulating CK
  • Cushing's Syndrome — Glucocorticoid excess causes muscle atrophy and reduced CK
  • Alcoholic Liver Disease — Chronic alcoholism may reduce CK with hepatic dysfunction
  • Advanced Age / Sarcopenia — Progressive muscle mass loss lowers baseline CK
  • Low Muscle Mass (Small stature, anorexia) — Less source tissue for CK production
  • Sample Inhibitors — Fluoride, citrate anticoagulants inhibit CK activity in vitro
Source: Henry's Clinical Diagnosis (24th ed.); Tietz Textbook of Clinical Chemistry (6th ed.); PMID: 24870125; ESC Guidelines on AMI, 2023
Section 08

Laboratory Testing Methods

Multiple methodologies are available for measuring total CK activity and quantifying specific isoenzymes. Method selection depends on clinical context, turnaround time requirements, and available instrumentation.

Fig.4 — CK Laboratory Testing: Spectrophotometric Assay (left) and CK Isoenzyme Electrophoresis Gel (right)

METHOD 01

Spectrophotometric (Kinetic UV) Assay

The reference method recommended by IFCC. Measures NADPH formation at 340 nm in the reverse direction (CK reverse reaction: PCr + ADP → Creatine + ATP). Coupled with hexokinase and G6PD reactions. High sensitivity and precision.

Gold Standard
METHOD 02

Electrophoresis (Agarose Gel / Cellulose Acetate)

Separates CK isoenzymes by charge: CK-BB migrates fastest (anodal), CK-MB intermediate, CK-MM slowest (cathodal). Bands visualized by fluorescence or colorimetric coupling. Used for definitive isoenzyme identification.

Isoenzyme Separation
METHOD 03

Immunoinhibition Assay

Anti-M antibody inhibits the M subunit activity; remaining activity is attributed to B subunit (CK-MB). Rapid and widely automated. Limited by cross-reactivity; may overestimate CK-MB in cases of macro-CK or CK-BB presence.

Automated / Rapid
METHOD 04

CK-MB Mass Immunoassay (ELISA / CLIA)

Quantifies CK-MB protein mass (ng/mL) using monoclonal antibodies against the M and B subunits. More specific than activity assay. Preferred for AMI diagnosis. Chemiluminescent immunoassay (CLIA) used on most analyzers.

Preferred for AMI
METHOD 05

Ion Exchange Chromatography / HPLC

High-resolution separation of CK isoenzymes and isoforms (including CK-MB1, CK-MB2 subforms). CK-MB2/CK-MB1 ratio >1.5 is an early marker of AMI (detects reperfusion within 2 hours). Research and specialized centers.

High Resolution

Section 09

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

Creatine Kinase is an enzyme of fundamental importance in both cellular physiology and clinical medicine. Its role as the primary ATP-buffering system in energy-demanding tissues makes it indispensable for normal muscle, cardiac, and neural function.

From a clinical laboratory perspective, CK and its isoenzymes provide organ-specific diagnostic windows. The CK-MB isoenzyme remains a cornerstone cardiac biomarker despite the rise of troponin assays. CK-MM elevation guides diagnosis of skeletal muscle disorders from rhabdomyolysis to muscular dystrophies. CK-BB, though rarely elevated in serum, signals significant CNS injury when detected.

Accurate interpretation requires knowledge of reference intervals, isoenzyme proportions, timing kinetics in disease states, and awareness of pre-analytical interferences. Serial measurement and correlation with clinical presentation remain the gold standard approach.
